I read the full draft in `md/01_introduction.md` through `md/10_conclusion.md`, plus the figure captions and tables. I did not rerun the experiments or verify external citations, so this is a manuscript-logic review rather than a replication.

The biggest problem is an overstrong central claim: **“Every LSE objective contains a descent direction, pure output scale”** is not true as written. It is true for cross-entropy only in the separated / correctly classified strict-margin regime, or under a precise aggregate derivative condition. Your own theory section correctly notes that scaling a misclassified example eventually increases loss without bound. That contradicts the introduction and conclusion, where the claim is stated globally. I would rewrite the thesis as something like: “For cross-entropy after separation, and for some LSE-family objectives with unpriced scale terms, positive output scaling is a hard-decision-preserving descent direction.” Less shiny, more defensible.

There is also a softmax-invariance error. Softmax responsibilities do **not** stay fixed under uniform scaling of logits; they are invariant to additive shifts, not multiplicative scaling. Scaling preserves the argmax and ordering for positive scale, but it changes probabilities, entropy, confidence, and the CE gradient. Relatedly, `02_theory.md` says “all relative logits are invariant” under `W → αW`; pairwise differences are not invariant, they scale by `α`. Only ordering, argmax, and perhaps ratios are preserved. This should be fixed because it sits near the conceptual core.

The draft repeatedly equates “accuracy is frozen” with “nothing structural changes.” That is too strong. Frozen accuracy only says hard predictions are unchanged on the measured set. The decision boundary, margins, representation geometry, robustness, and near-boundary behavior can still change. Your own E1 result says the full-batch gradient is only about one-third radial late in training, so there is still tangential refinement. The safer statement is: “A large share of late final-head displacement is hard-prediction-preserving scale motion.”

The “missing volume term” framing is rhetorically powerful but currently overextended. The draft moves from “these literatures all observe norm/confidence scale pathologies” to “one missing volume term, four names.” That is a leap unless you formally derive the same dropped log-determinant/volume term for each objective and setting. As written, the paper has strong evidence for a shared **scale degree of freedom**, but weaker evidence for a single literal **missing volume term** explaining calibration, OOD logit norms, implicit bias, metric learning, and the SAE anomaly.

The “responsibility-weighted majority” condition in E1 is not precise enough. The radial derivative for CE is controlled by the sum of terms like `E_p[z] - z_y`, not by a majority vote of examples. A small number of high-loss or high-margin wrong examples can dominate many correct examples. Replace “responsibility-weighted majority correctly classified” with an exact scalar condition on the batch radial derivative.

The SAE closed-form argument has a hidden assumption. `05_e2b_sae.md` says ReLU sparsity pins `min_j a_ij = 0` per example, so the LSE term saturates. ReLU ensures nonnegativity, but it does not guarantee that every example has at least one inactive unit. If all units are positive for an example, `log Σ exp(-αa_ij)` goes like `-α min_j a_ij`, not a constant. Maybe your trained SAE empirically has at least one zero activation per example; if so, state and measure that. Otherwise the “closed form” is conditional, not exact.

The calibration section has a possible methodological landmine: the post-hoc optimal temperature `T*`. If `T*` was chosen on the test set and then test ECE is reported, the “one global scalar repairs 63–90%” result is optimistic and should not be presented as a deployed calibration claim. If it was chosen on a held-out validation set and evaluated on test, say that explicitly. This is important enough to affect the credibility of E4.

The phrase “fixes calibrate exactly insofar as they move the deployed confidence scale toward the calibrated value” is too absolute, and your own later paragraph weakens it. You show a dominant global-temperature component plus a shape residual; label smoothing especially has a larger irreducible residual. So “exactly insofar” should become “largely insofar,” “to first order,” or “in these experiments, most ECE is removable by a scalar temperature.”

The train-time temperature arm is conceptually tricky. A jointly learned temperature under CE is not the same object as post-hoc temperature scaling, because it is non-identifiable with logit scale: weights and temperature can compensate for each other. Freezing it halfway is an intervention, but it is not a standard calibration fix. The paper can still use it, but it should explicitly say this arm is a diagnostic scale-relocation experiment, not ordinary temperature scaling.

The LogitNorm claim needs a deployment-convention check. You argue that LogitNorm prices the loss-side scale but leaves raw deployed logits unpriced. That depends on whether the intended deployed model uses raw logits or normalized logits. If the baseline LogitNorm method evaluates normalized logits at inference, then raw-logit ECE is evaluating the wrong function. If raw logits are indeed the intended deployment path, say so and cite it.

The E2 under-confidence claim is not supported by ECE alone. The full arm has worse ECE and lower accuracy, but ECE is unsigned. To claim under-confidence, show signed calibration error, reliability diagrams, mean confidence vs accuracy, or optimal `T* < 1`. E4 has that kind of sign evidence; E2 as drafted does not.

The optimizer mechanism claim is interesting but too broad. “Which mechanism dominates is selected by the objective’s radial payout, not by the optimizer alone” is supported by two testbeds: CE on MNIST and the SAE objective. That supports a hypothesis or observed contrast, not a general selection principle. The wording should be “in these testbeds” unless you add theory or more objectives.

Statistical language is stronger than the evidence shown. Several places say “statistically indistinguishable,” “best all-round,” “exactly,” or “dominant” with only three seeds and limited uncertainty reporting. E4’s Spearman result uses only six arms, so any rank-correlation claim should be presented cautiously. The limitations section admits the scale issue, but the body should not speak more generally than the design supports.

Small but concrete wording/math issues: `02_theory.md` says the minibatch estimator is “≈ 4× chance = 1/Kd”; it should say the observed value is about 4× the chance baseline, where chance is `1/Kd`. `08_related_work.md` says you measure magnification’s “share of the step budget,” but E1 says per-step minibatch radial share is noise-floor; what you mainly measure is epoch displacement/travel. The draft alternates between raw and deployed ECE for the temperature arm; make sure every headline number uses the deployed function. “Five regularizers” plus baseline plus temperature should be described consistently.

The repo references that do not belong in a paper are easy cleanup: `notes/predictions.md`, `src/test_decomposition.py`, `src/e4_recompute_ece.py`, figure filename links like `fig_e1_estimators.png`, the index file’s repo layout comments, and “the original repository’s documented −999 ± 1.” Move preregistration, tests, and scripts to an appendix, artifact note, OSF link, anonymous supplement, or reproducibility statement.

My suggested core rewrite is: the paper is strongest when it claims **a measurable hard-decision-preserving scale channel in separated CE training, plus an unbounded scale payout in the SAE objective, with interventions showing that calibration and loss can be substantially controlled by pricing that scale**. It is weakest when it says **every LSE objective**, **nothing structural changes**, or **one missing volume term explains four literatures**. Those are goblin-shiny claims, but they need either narrower wording or more proof.
