## Part 1 — Must fix before anyone else reads this

### 1.1 The invariance claim in §2.3 is still wrong (third time flagged)

`02_theory.md:49-50` says "argmax, logit ordering, and all relative logits
are invariant under W → αW." Logit *differences* — which is what "relative
logits" means to most readers — are NOT invariant: z_i − z_j scales by α.
What is invariant: argmax, ordering, and logit *ratios* z_i/z_j. Both prior
reviews flagged this. Write exactly: "argmax, logit ordering, and logit
ratios are invariant; logit differences scale by α, which is precisely the
confidence channel." That last clause turns the correction into content.
While you're there, `01_introduction.md:12` ("responsibilities depend only on
relative outputs") has the same disease: softmax depends on logit
*differences*, and differences are not scale-invariant — responsibilities
sharpening under scale is the entire paper. Say "responsibilities are
invariant under shifts of z; the argmax is invariant under positive scaling;
the responsibilities themselves are not" — do not let the intro use a loose
phrase whose precise version is your own mechanism.

### 1.2 Three papers you must cite and differentiate, or a referee does it for you

- **Bai et al., "A Geometric Perspective towards Neural Calibration via
  Sensitivity Decomposition" (arXiv:2110.14577).** Decomposes softmax
  behavior into norm and angular components and connects norm growth to
  miscalibration. This is the closest thing to your decomposition applied to
  your E4 problem. Your differentiators are real — exact generator in
  parameter space, the estimator hierarchy, causal intervention, the
  optimizer split — but you must state them against this paper explicitly in
  §8, or the first reviewer who knows it writes "prior work, uncited."
- **Heo et al., "AdamP" (arXiv:2006.08217, ICLR 2021).** They observed that
  momentum optimizers induce excessive norm growth on scale-invariant weights
  and *built an optimizer that projects out the radial component of the
  update*. Your E2 intervention exists there as engineering; your E3
  observation half-exists there. Your differentiator: they treat
  BN-scale-invariant weights (loss doesn't change along the radial direction);
  you treat the softmax head, where the radial direction is a *descent*
  direction — opposite sign of the same geometry. That contrast is actually a
  nice paragraph. Write it.
- **Poggio et al., "Complexity control by gradient descent in deep networks"
  (Nature Comm. 2020).** States norm divergence under separability with only
  normalized outputs mattering. Belongs in the implicit-bias paragraph of §8
  alongside Soudry/Ji–Telgarsky/Lyu–Li.

Also consider **Wan et al., "Spherical Motion Dynamics"** — it models the
equilibrium between weight decay and gradient-induced norm growth for
scale-invariant weights, which is the closest existing theory to your §2.5
diffusion channel and directly relevant to your "diffusive norm growth lacks
a theory" open problem in §9. Citing it converts a gap into a research
program.

### 1.3 "None cites the others" is false — and it's your opening move

`01_introduction.md:20-21`: Mukhoti et al. cite Guo et al.; Wei et al. cite
the calibration literature; Bai et al. straddle two of your four communities.
The defensible claim is: "these literatures each treat the symptom locally;
none derives it from a common mechanism or measures its share of training
motion." That is still a strong hook and it is true. The current sentence is
a one-line desk-reject risk because it is checkable in thirty seconds.

### 1.4 State the T* protocol in §7

`07_e4_fixes.md:81-88`: the 63–90% removable numbers are your strongest
unification evidence, and the draft never says what split T* was fit on. One
sentence: "T* minimizes ECE on the test set, following Guo et al.; it
characterizes the structure of the miscalibration, not a deployable method."
If it was fit on test, say so plainly — the *diagnostic* use is legitimate,
the *silence* is not. If you can cheaply refit on a held-out split, do it and
report both; the numbers will barely move and the objection dies forever.

### 1.5 E2's under-confidence claim needs its own signed evidence

`04_e2_intervention.md:60-62` asserts the full arm is "miscalibrated in the
under-confident direction." ECE is unsigned; the assertion is currently
theory, not measurement. You already have the tool: run
`e4_recompute_ece.py`-style T* on the E2 full-arm checkpoints and report
T* < 1, or add a one-line reliability statement (mean confidence < accuracy).
Note that E4's T* = 0.79 belongs to label smoothing and cannot be borrowed
here. This is an afternoon of work.

### 1.6 Verify the ReLU sparsity premise of the E2b closed form

`05_e2b_sae.md:16-18`: "ReLU sparsity pins min_j a_ij = 0 per example" is the
load-bearing premise of the LSE-saturation step, asserted, never measured.
Add one measured sentence: "Across all trained models, ≥ X% of examples have
at least one inactive unit (mean m inactive)." If it is not 100%, scope the
closed form to the examples where it holds and say what fraction that is.
Your exactness claim ("to three significant figures") deserves an exact
premise.

### 1.7 Scope the central claim — four words

`01_introduction.md:13-15` and `10_conclusion.md:3` state "every LSE
objective contains a descent direction" unconditionally; §2.2 itself proves
it is conditional (pre-separation the radial direction is not pure descent).
Add "once the responsibility-weighted majority is correctly classified" (or
simply "post-separation") in both places. E2b is your excuse for keeping the
claim broad — there the payout is unconditional — so you may alternatively
say "a descent direction whose payout the objective selects," but pick one
and make intro, theory, and conclusion agree.

### 1.8 Get the failure count consistent

`01_introduction.md:63-64` says three predictions failed (P2, P9, P10);
`10_conclusion.md:23` says "three of twelve." The reports also record P11a
half-falsified (LogitNorm increased R) and P11c falsified (correlation did
not improve ex-temperature). Either the count is five-ish or you define
"failed" as "headline prediction refuted" and say so. A reviewer
cross-checking `notes/predictions.md` against the intro should find zero
discrepancies — the pre-registration story is your credibility asset, so an
inconsistent tally is disproportionately expensive.

## Part 2 — Structural advice (do this before polishing prose)

### 2.1 E3 is your best result. The paper doesn't know it.

The advection/diffusion split with objective-selected transport
(`06_e3_optimizers.md`) is the one claim I could not find anywhere in the
literature, including the three papers in 1.2. It is currently contribution
#4 of 5 and section 6 of 10. Two options, in order of my preference:

1. Restructure the intro's contribution list around it: instrument (E1) →
   intervention and leakage (E2) → **transport mechanisms and their selection
   by the objective (E3 + E2b, together)** → fix taxonomy (E4). E2b is
   evidence *for* the E3 selection principle; presenting them as one
   contribution strengthens both and de-emphasizes the five-paper predecessor
   scaffold.
2. At minimum, move the E3 claim to contribution #2 and put "to our knowledge
   this plot does not exist" language in the intro rather than only at
   `06_e3_optimizers.md:63-66`.

### 2.2 Demote the implicit-EM framing to where it pays rent

The EM reading appears in the intro (`01:16-18`), §2.6, §8, and the
conclusion's second paragraph. It requires a reviewer to accept two
predecessor arXiv papers to parse "missing log-determinant volume term," and
E1–E4 stand entirely without it. §2.6 already contains the right sentence:
"it is the series' through-line, not this paper's main road" — but the intro
and conclusion don't obey it. Concretely: cut the EM sentence from intro
paragraph 2 (keep it in a remark or footnote), keep §2.6 as is, let E2b be
the place the volume term becomes literal, and in the conclusion make
"missing volume term" explicitly an *interpretation* offered after the
measured claims, not the frame they hang from. You lose nothing a referee
would credit and you shed the grand-unified-theory pattern-match.

### 2.3 Reorganize §8 as unification-first

`08_related_work.md` is organized literature-by-literature, each paragraph
ending in "what we add" — a defensive crouch. Invert it: open with one
paragraph stating the shared observation (norm/scale grows post-separation;
confidence sharpens; accuracy doesn't move) with all four communities cited
in a single sentence, then one paragraph per community on what they fixed
and what the fix looks like in your decomposition (temperature = global
radial reposition; LogitNorm = loss-side pricing; weight decay = inward
radial force; sphere methods = manifold projection). The Bai/AdamP/Poggio
citations from 1.2 slot naturally into this structure. Keep the citation-
hygiene TODO at `08:63-66` — good habit — but resolve it before freeze.

### 2.4 Soften "exactly insofar" — your own table contradicts it

`01_introduction.md:56` and `07_e4_fixes.md:43-44`: "fixes calibrate exactly
insofar as they move the deployed confidence scale toward the calibrated
value." Your Figure 6 shows equal scale gaps with different ECE
(temperature 0.050 vs LogitNorm 0.081) and a scale-irreparable label-smoothing
residual — §7's own third finding refutes "exactly." The final-form claim at
`07:114-117` (dominant scale component + arm-specific shape residual) is
correct and better; make the intro's contribution #5 quote *that* claim, not
the slogan version it walks back 60 lines later.

### 2.5 Give the R column a defined window

`07_e4_fixes.md` table reports R = 14/83/155/167/544/45825 while the lab
report's epochs-≥40 numbers are 4.8/45.2/88.7/101.8/261.6/1264.2. Figure 5
panel B excludes the first 40 epochs; the table apparently doesn't. State the
integration window in the table caption, and consider making the table match
panel B since you argue the transient is uninformative — right now a careful
reader reconciling table, figure, and report gets three different LogitNorm
numbers.

## Part 3 — Section-by-section notes (smaller, still worth doing)

**§1 Introduction.** The opening paragraph is excellent — three curves
disagreeing is the best cold open in the series. Keep it. "One missing volume
term, four names" (`01:28`) — cut or demote per 2.2; "one degree of freedom,
four names" preserves the rhythm without the EM toll. Contribution #2's
"leakage theorem-by-counterexample" is cute but oversells — Lyu–Li already
provides the theorem; you provide the demonstration that an optimizer *finds*
the leak. Say that.

**§2 Theory.** §2.4's "where the optimizer wanders vs where it travels" is a
genuinely good sentence and the estimator hierarchy is the section's real
contribution — consider giving the hierarchy a display equation or small
table (estimator → object → question answered) so reviewers can cite it.
§2.2: "E1 refines this binary statement" is good forward reference; also
state the refined version here in one sentence so the theory section is
self-contained. The invariants two-list paragraph (`02:66-70`) duplicates
theory.md's table — a table is better in the paper too.

**§3 E1.** Strong section. The split verdict on P4 is handled honestly.
One gap: the caption promises "3 seeds" but only the drift slopes are
reported per-seed; give mean ± std for ρ_disp 0.846 and the gap 0.006
somewhere (even a footnote), since "≥3 seeds, mean ± std" is your own stated
convention. "This plot is the paper" was the README's bar — E1 met it;
consider saying in one sentence why the smooth rise (P2's failure) makes the
*instrument* more useful, not less: it detects radial payout before full
separation, which the binary theory would have missed.

**§4 E2.** The projection-vs-reparametrization paragraph (`04:17-21`) is
exactly the kind of preemption reviewers reward — good. Table: add ± std
(you have 3 seeds; the ECE differences you interpret are a few 1e-3, so the
reader needs the noise scale to believe 0.0033 vs 0.0092). The full-arm
lr-transfer caveat lives in §9 (`09:39-43`) but belongs in this section too —
one sentence at the full-arm paragraph, since that's where the reader forms
the belief.

**§5 E2b.** Tightest section in the paper; the scale-accounting
(loss + 2K log(norm ratio) landing both optimizers in the same range) is the
single most convincing paragraph in the draft. Fix 1.6. Also: SGD baseline
−777 ± 45 has 75× the variance of Adam's ±0.6 — one clause on why (SGD's
scale climb is noise-limited?) would preempt the obvious question. Seeds
{1,2,3} here vs {0,1,2} elsewhere — harmless, but a referee may ask; a
footnote "matching the predecessor repo's seeds" kills it if true.

**§6 E3.** Your best writing and your best result. "It exists only because a
registered prediction was allowed to fail in public" (`06:66`) — keep, it's
the thesis of your methodology. Add AdamP contrast here (per 1.2): their
projection removes what your SGD channel does, yet Adam's diffusion is
exactly what their projection can't see — one sentence makes your novelty
legible against the nearest neighbor. The claim "neither the radial-share
plot nor the selection principle appears in the literatures" — after 1.2's
citations, re-verify and rescope to the selection principle, which is the
part I believe survives.

**§7 E4.** The deployment-corrected recomputation rescuing P11b is a good
story — but be explicit that Figure 5's ECE column and Figure 6's deployed
ECE differ for the temperature arm only because the raw evaluation was
wrong; a reader currently has to infer which table is authoritative. Mark
the Figure 5 temperature row more loudly than an asterisk, or drop raw ECE
for that arm entirely. The stale-temperature moral (`07:93-97`) is a
publishable observation on its own — make sure it appears in the intro or
conclusion, it currently lives only here.

**§9 Limitations.** Honest and complete for what it covers. Add the missing
one: **no transformer, no LLM-scale run** — norm growth is documented in
transformer pretraining, AdamW is the default there, and your
AdamW-cuts-diffusion finding is directly testable. Naming it as the natural
next experiment costs one sentence and preempts the "2019-scale evidence"
review; leaving it unnamed looks like you didn't notice.

**§10 Conclusion.** "Each failure bought the paper its sharpest result" —
good closing move. Fix the count (1.8). Final sentence's "sufficient to
explain the anomalies it created" — "sufficient" is doing unsupported work
(E4's shape residual is precisely the insufficiency); "measurable, removable,
and priced" matches your own E4 conclusion better.

**Title.** Take the conservative candidate or a hybrid. "Every Log-Sum-Exp
Objective Pays for Confidence It Didn't Earn" as a *subtitle* is charming;
as a title it promises a theorem the paper scopes down in §2.2. "Error
Juicing: A Radial/Tangential Decomposition of Training Dynamics in
Log-Sum-Exp Objectives" keeps the brand and the precision.
